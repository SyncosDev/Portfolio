#1 Course Lesson

Japanese might appear as a difficult langauge. Don't get me wrong it is. But giving up is not necessary as learning any language will be challenging at the start. Don't letter the multiple alphabets and unknown characters scare you off because I believe anyone can learn it, if they put their mind to it.

To improve as much as possible while using this course you'll have to constantly imerse yourself. Learning words and characters and words without using them often is useless. Therefore reading books and watching movies is a great way to both pick up the language and refresh the vocabulary.

The first step to mastering the japanese language is learning the alphabets. Don't worry! We'll only start with Hiragana and then introduce Katakana and Kanji later on. This lesson will span roughly a month to make sure the characters stay before moving on. There will be information about each row of characters and then pratice exercises related to the information you learn't. This will basically work like school but instead of focusing on presenting a powerpoint presentation. Hikari Nihongo focuses on making the user to put the knowledge to use as fast as possible because it stays better when you do it and understand it. So let's get started with the first lesson addiontal goodluck on your journey to fluency. I'll provide as much help as I can to make sure you succeed.

Hiragana

Hiragana is thbe alphabet in Japanese used for native words connected to Japan. The alphabet is often divided into 10 different rows of characters with 3 addiontal characters that work a bit differently compared to any of the other rows. The exercises below will be about the first row, also called the vowels of the alphabet. They consist of the sounds of a, i, u, e, o.

The First Row

The first row of the hiragana alphabet consist of the vowel sounds. They're prounuced like the letters a, i, u, e and o. Each one of them have their own stroke order that technically doesn't mean anything other then the fact someone thought it was the best way to draw them back in the day. Because of this we'll learn writing them correctly already from the start.

The vowels doesn't only serve the purpose of being letters. They can also create something called longer vowels. This means that adding a vowel in front of another hiragana characters creates a long sound instead of the regular. This might appear as a small feature but is the only way to hear the differece between certain words. Saying the wrong word might leave you in a bit of trouble.

The picture below shows the characters but not the stroke order. Learning the stroke is something you will do unconsiousnly while just trying to draw them in the exercises. So don't worry about that. In the picture there is also a few words that might be usefull later on in your journey but take it as homework to memorize these at the same time as learning the characters. The words only require the vowels to be spelled and therefore is a perfect match.

Now that you know the basics of what's coming it's time to start doing some exercises. The first part will consist of writing the letter that match the shown character in the small box. The second phase will be the flipped where you will not write but draw the character using your mouse. This will guide you to do it in the correct stroke order so just follow the instructions and you'll be fine. Goodluck with your first exercise.
